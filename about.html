About

Engineer turned designer, still solving problems the same way

I spend most of my time on the problems other portfolios skip: the person grieving, the neighbor who's tuned out local government, the mind that processes the world differently. Good design starts with taking them seriously.

How I got here

I came to product design from an engineering background, which means I still think in systems and constraints before I think in pixels. That habit turned out to be useful: most design problems are actually structural problems wearing a visual costume. Fix the structure — the flow, the hierarchy, the moment of trust — and the interface tends to follow.

What keeps me interested is designing for people who are usually an afterthought in tech: people navigating loss, people disengaged from civic life, people whose sensory experience of the world doesn't match the default assumptions baked into most products. Those aren't edge cases to me — they're the actual test of whether a design works.

How I work

  1. Understand the real problem

    Interviews and context-gathering before anything gets drawn — I want to know what people are actually trying to do, not what the brief assumes.

  2. Define what "working" means

    Turning research into a clear point of view: the one or two things the design has to get right, and what I'm deliberately choosing not to solve.

  3. Design in systems, not screens

    Every screen inherits rules — spacing, type, color logic — so the product feels considered as a whole, not assembled one page at a time.

  4. Test, then cut

    Putting work in front of real users and being willing to remove what doesn't hold up, even if it was hard to make.
