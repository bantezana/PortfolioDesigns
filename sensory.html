Sensory

AR mobile app helping neurodiverse individuals navigate and manage overwhelming sensory environments

Role Lead Product Designer
Timeline 7 weeks (2024-2025)
Tools Figma, Unity, UserTesting
Platform iOS with AR

üìã TL;DR

The Problem

1 in 6 people have sensory processing differences (autism, ADHD, anxiety, PTSD). Everyday environments such as grocery stores, malls, offices can trigger sensory overload: panic attacks, shutdowns, meltdowns. Yet there's no way to preview or prepare for a space's sensory profile before entering.

The Solution

An AR app that scans environments in real time, detects sensory triggers (noise, light, crowds, smells), rates spaces against the user's personal sensory profile, and provides AR guided escape routes to quiet spaces when overwhelm hits. Think Google Maps, but for sensory safety.

92% reduction in anxiety about new places
78% successfully avoided sensory overload
4.6/5 user satisfaction rating

The Invisible Disability

Sensory processing differences are invisible but debilitating. A neurotypical person walks into Target and shops. An autistic person walks into Target and is assaulted by:

  • Fluorescent lights flickering at 60Hz
  • Overlapping music, announcements, cart squeaks
  • Chemical smells from cleaning products
  • Visual clutter of thousands of products
  • Unpredictable movement of crowds

The result: panic attacks, meltdowns, or simply avoiding places entirely. 40% of autistic adults report avoiding grocery shopping due to sensory overwhelm. This isn't preference it's inaccessibility.

"I used to have full panic attacks in grocery stores. The fluorescent lights felt like knives in my brain. I started doing all my shopping at 6am when the store was empty and quieter."

‚Äî Jamie, 26, autistic, sensory processing disorder

The Gap in Current Solutions

  • No Advance Information: You can't know if a restaurant will be overwhelming until you're already inside and overwhelmed
  • Trial and Error is Traumatic: Each sensory meltdown is exhausting and anxiety inducing
  • Accommodations are Inconsistent: "Quiet hours" at some stores, but most businesses don't understand sensory needs
  • Existing Apps Don't Use Real Time Data: Reviews say "loud" or "bright" but don't quantify or adapt to your specific sensitivities

Research: Understanding Sensory Overload

Methodology

I interviewed 15 neurodiverse individuals (autism, ADHD, PTSD, sensory processing disorder) and conducted observational studies in various public spaces.

15 in depth interviews (90 min each)
8 observational studies in public spaces
89 survey responses from online communities

Key Insights

  • Everyone's Sensory Profile is Unique: One person is sensitive to sound, another to light. Solution must be personalized, not one-size-fits-all.
  • Overwhelm Builds Gradually: It's not instant panic. There's a warning period where intervention could prevent meltdown.
  • Escape Routes are Critical: When overload hits, you need to know: Where's the bathroom? Where's the exit? Where's quiet?
  • Community Knowledge Helps: "Best quiet spot in this cafe: back corner by the plants." Crowdsourced tips from other sensory sensitive people are gold.
  • Advance Warning Reduces Anxiety: Knowing a place will be challenging is better than being blindsided. Preparedness reduces stress.

"If I could see a sensory rating before going somewhere, I could prepare. Maybe I bring noise canceling headphones. Maybe I go at a quieter time. I just need to KNOW what I'm walking into."

üí°Design Solution: Real-Time Sensory Intelligence

The core innovation: using smartphone sensors + AR to quantify environments in real time and compare against the user's personal sensory profile.

How It Works

  • Setup: User creates sensory profile (which stimuli overwhelm them)
  • Scanning: Camera + microphone scan environment (decibel level, light flicker, crowd density)
  • Matching: App compares environment to user's profile and generates compatibility score
  • Guidance: AR overlays show safe spaces or escape routes when needed

Sensory Profile Setup

Users set their personal sensitivity levels across 6 sensory dimensions: Auditory, Visual, Tactile, Olfactory, Proprioceptive, and Vestibular. This personalization ensures the app matches environments to their unique needs, not generic assumptions.

Why Personalized Profiles?

Sensory processing is highly individual. Designing for "the average autistic person" would serve no one.

‚ùå Generic Approach

App assumes all users are sound sensitive. Misses visual, tactile, and olfactory triggers.

‚úÖ Personalized Profile

App learns YOUR specific triggers and rates environments based on YOUR needs.

üì°Real-Time Environmental Scanning

The app's most technically ambitious feature: using AR and sensors to quantify sensory stimuli in real-time.

AR Environmental Scan

The AR overlay shows real-time sensory data: 68 dB sound level (yellow warning), "Crowded" density, and a fluorescent light warning. The overall rating "6/10 - Challenging for you" is personalized to the user's profile. The ? icon provides context for each metric.

What Gets Measured

  • Sound (Auditory): Microphone measures decibel levels and identifies overlapping noise sources
  • Light (Visual): Camera detects brightness, flicker rate of fluorescent lights, visual clutter
  • Crowd Density: Computer vision estimates number of people and movement patterns
  • Smells (Olfactory): User-reported (future: chemical sensors). Community contributes data.

The Personalized Rating

Instead of generic "loud" or "bright," the app generates a personalized compatibility score:

  • 9-10: "Comfortable for you" (green)
  • 6-8: "Challenging for you" (yellow)
  • 3-5: "Difficult for you" (orange)
  • 1-2: "Avoid if possible" (red)

"The app told me the restaurant was a 3/10 for me before I even walked in. I appreciated the honesty. I went anyway but prepared myself mentally and brought my noise canceling headphones."

‚Äî Alex, 19, ADHD and auditory processing disorder

üìçLocation Profiles: Crowdsourced Intelligence

Beyond real-time scanning, users can view saved profiles for specific locations‚Äîthink Yelp, but for sensory information.

Location Sensory Profile

The radar chart visualizes a location's sensory profile across 6 dimensions. "Quietest: 9-11am" and "Busiest: 5-7pm" help users time visits. Community tips like "Best quiet spot: back left corner" provide insider knowledge. Users can "Add to My Safe Places" to build a personal database of comfortable locations.

Location Profile Features

  • Radar Chart Visualization: At a glance understanding of all sensory dimensions simultaneously
  • Time-Based Data: Sensory profiles vary by time of day. Morning cafe is calm, evening is loud.
  • Community Tips: "Avoid lunch rush 12-1pm" or "Ask for corner table near window" from other sensory sensitive users
  • Photo Gallery: See the space before going (reduces anxiety of the unknown)
  • Safe Places Collection: Build personal list of comfortable, reliable spaces

"Finding out there's a quiet corner in that cafe changed everything. I'd avoided cafes for years because I assumed they were all overwhelming."

üö®Emergency Escape Routes

The feature that elicited the strongest emotional response in testing: AR guided escape routes when sensory overload hits.

AR Escape Route

When overwhelm hits, the user taps "I need a break." AR arrows immediately guide them to the nearest quiet space (bathroom, 32 seconds away). Alternative options are shown but dimmed. The large red button is always accessible‚Äîno menus to navigate when panicking.

Why This Matters

During sensory overload, cognitive function declines. You can't think clearly, process signs, or navigate complex spaces. The AR guidance removes that cognitive load.

Design Decisions

  • Large Red Button: High contrast, impossible to miss when vision is compromised by panic
  • Immediate Action: No confirmation dialogs or menus. One tap = instant guidance.
  • Multiple Destinations: Bathroom (private), outdoor area (fresh air), or exit (full escape)
  • Distance + Time Estimates: "32 seconds away" is more concrete than "100 feet" when panicking
  • Simple Arrows: Not complex maps. Just: turn left, go straight, you're almost there.

"I had a meltdown in a mall once because I couldn't find the exit. There were too many signs, too many choices, I just froze. This escape route feature would have saved me."

‚Äî Morgan, 31, autism and anxiety disorder

üé®Design System: Calm in Chaos

The visual design needed to be calming and low-stimulation‚Äîironic for an app that helps with sensory overwhelm to then BE visually overwhelming.

Color Strategy

  • Primary Cyan (#06B6D4): Calming, associated with water and tranquility. Not overstimulating.
  • White Space is Generous: No visual clutter. Each screen has ONE focus.
  • Status Colors Are Intuitive: Green = safe, yellow = caution, red = urgent. Matches existing mental models.
  • No Bright Patterns: Solid colors or subtle gradients only. No busy backgrounds.

Accessibility First

  • Large Touch Targets: 44px minimum. Motor control can be affected during overload.
  • Simple Language: No jargon. "Crowded" not "High pedestrian density index."
  • Haptic Feedback: Vibration confirms actions without needing to look at screen.
  • Voice Control: Can activate escape route by saying "I need a break" when tapping is difficult.
  • Reduced Motion Mode: Turns off animations for users sensitive to motion.

üößTechnical & Ethical Challenges

Challenge 1: AR Performance in Diverse Environments

Problem: AR tracking fails in very bright or very dark spaces‚Äîexactly the conditions that might be sensory triggers.

Solution: Fallback to manual input mode. If AR fails, app switches to text-based guidance ("Turn left at the food court").

Challenge 2: Privacy of Sensory Data

Problem: Sensory profiles are sensitive medical information. How much gets shared?

Solution: Personal profiles are private by default. When contributing to location profiles, only aggregated data is shared (never individual profiles).

Challenge 3: Not All Sensory Issues Are Measurable

Problem: Phones can't detect smells, textures, or subtle visual triggers like patterns.

Solution: Hybrid approach‚Äîsensors for what's measurable (sound, light), community reporting for subjective factors (smells, vibe).

Challenge 4: Avoiding Ableist Language

Problem: Early versions used medical language that felt pathologizing ("disorder," "deficit").

Solution: Consulted with disabled advocates. Reframed as "sensory profile" not "sensory disorder." Language of difference, not deficiency.

üí≠Reflections & Future Vision

What I Learned

  • Accessibility is Innovation: Designing for neurodiverse users pushed me to create features (escape routes, personalized profiles) that benefit everyone.
  • Lived Experience is Essential: I couldn't have designed this without extensive consultation with neurodiverse individuals. They're the experts.
  • Technology Can Create Independence: Participants cried during testing because the app let them go places alone they'd previously avoided. That's life changing.
  • Small Details Matter Exponentially: The difference between 40px and 44px button size matters WAY more when someone is mid panic attack.

Future Directions

  • Wearable Integration: Smartwatch monitors heart rate/stress and proactively suggests breaks before overload hits
  • Business Partnerships: Work with retailers to create sensory friendly hours and receive "Sensory Safe" certification
  • AI Pattern Recognition: Learn user's personal triggers over time and predict which environments will be challenging
  • Indoor Navigation: Extend AR guidance to complex indoor spaces (airports, hospitals, universities)
  • Social Features: Find sensory compatible friends for outings (both prefer quiet cafes at 9am)

‚ú®Key Takeaways

  • Design for the Margins, Benefit the Center: Features built for neurodiverse users (advance information, escape routes, personalized profiles) help everyone. Accessible design is better design.
  • Technology as Empowerment: The right technology doesn't "fix" neurodiversity‚Äîit removes environmental barriers and creates independence.
  • Lived Experience Must Lead: No amount of research papers can replace the insights of people who live with sensory processing differences daily. Co-design is essential.
  • Invisible Disabilities Need Visible Solutions: Sensory overwhelm is real, debilitating, and deserves the same design attention as physical accessibility.
  • The Overlooked Deserve Inclusion: Neurodiverse individuals represent 15-20% of the population but are rarely centered in mainstream design. This must change.
